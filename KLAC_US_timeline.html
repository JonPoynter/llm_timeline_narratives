2026 AI infrastructure ramp: demand, constraints, and capital returns

2026-01-29 → 2026-06-27
Through the first half of 2026, KLA linked record performance and strong outlook to the global AI infrastructure buildout across foundry/logic, memory, advanced packaging, and services. Management sized 2026 WFE in the mid‑$130Bs framework (including AP), with China trending up for KLA and DRAM outgrowing foundry/logic. Services scaled with double‑digit growth drivers, and guidance called for stronger 2H26 versus 1H26. At the same time, long lead times, supply bottlenecks (e.g., complex optics), and component cost/tariff headwinds tempered near‑term upside. Management emphasized value‑based pricing rather than scarcity pricing, and noted urgency around 2027 capacity as new greenfield ramps approach. KLA authorized an additional $7B buyback and raised its quarterly dividend, citing confidence tied to AI‑related momentum and process control share gains. Internally, KLA continued to build AI capabilities, seeking engineers to develop LLM-powered applications leveraging multi‑provider platforms (e.g., Claude, OpenAI) on Azure and Bedrock. Regulatory themes persisted, including evolving U.S. export controls and a government letter regarding China with immaterial near‑term impact but a slower growth trajectory than overall WFE.

Cloud groundwork and internal AI adoption

2022-04-07 → 2025-09-08
KLA began laying a cloud and AI foundation ahead of the 2026 AI infrastructure cycle. In April 2022, the company moved its Frontline PCB design-for-manufacturability analysis to a cloud-based SaaS model, citing materially faster turnarounds on compute‑intensive workloads. This represented a shift of key engineering software to hyperscale compute and positioned KLA’s software customers to benefit from elastic cloud capacity. By 2025, KLA disclosed operational use of AI in recruiting, including chatbots and algorithmic candidate matching supported by third-party service providers. While framed as efficiency-enhancing and subject to human decision-making, the notice acknowledged data sharing for training and refinement of AI algorithms, introducing governance considerations tied to privacy and model training data. Together these elements show KLA both productizing cloud-native workflows for customers and adopting AI internally, though without naming any one frontier LLM provider as a core dependency during this period.
