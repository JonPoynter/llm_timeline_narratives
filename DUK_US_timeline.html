ESA-driven large-load (data center) pipeline and financing/regulatory support

2026-05-05 → 2026-05-05
Duke reported a late‑stage ESA pipeline of 15.4 GW with 7.6 GW executed, expecting initial energy deliveries as early as 2H27 and ramps into the 2030s. Management emphasized gating factors (zoning/permitting), noted about two‑thirds of executed ESAs are already under construction, and is exploring bridge power options to accelerate service. The total prospect pipeline exceeds the disclosed late‑stage figure, with rising interest across Charlotte, Florida, and southern Indiana, and more conversions anticipated over the next 12 months. To support growth, Duke reaffirmed a multi‑year ~$103B capex plan and described funding levers (asset monetizations, convertibles, ATM, tax credit monetization) alongside executing a multi‑year forward tax credit sale. Regulatory tailwinds include South Carolina’s approval of a ~1.365 GW combined‑cycle gas plant and efforts to codify large‑load protections via tariffs. Nuclear life‑extension remains valuable (~$600M/yr tax credits). Risks persist around ESA conversion timing and on‑time, on‑budget grid/generation delivery given supply chain and labor constraints.

Foundational renewable tariff groundwork and first hyperscaler program use

2013-11-15 → 2015-11-25
Duke Energy Carolinas initiated and secured approval for the Green Source Rider (GSR), an experimental renewable program tailored for energy‑intensive customers including data centers. North Carolina regulators approved the tariff in December 2013, enabling large customers to offset new load with renewables. In 2015, Google became the first buyer under the program to support its Lenoir, NC data center with a 61 MW solar project via Duke’s GSR construct. This established an early template for working with hyperscalers on dedicated clean energy solutions tied to data center expansion in Duke’s service territory.
