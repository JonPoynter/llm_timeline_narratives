NBM scale-out, backlog expansion, and AI-led datacenter surge

2026-04-30 → 2026-06-08
By fiscal Q3 2026, SanDisk had signed five multi-year NBM agreements (three in Q3 and two in Q4-to-date), with CFO-quantified RPO of about $42B for the three Q3-signed deals, supported by approximately $11B of financial guarantees and roughly $400M of customer prepayments already on the balance sheet. Management indicated over a third of FY27 bits were already under NBM, with ambitions to exceed 50% over time. The company lifted its CY26 data center growth assumption to the mid‑70s%, tied to strong AI-led demand, and reported Q3 Datacenter revenue of $1.467B (+233% q/q; +645% y/y). Product milestones included expectations for QLC ‘Stargate’ revenue shipments in Q4, a TLC/QLC mix update, and continued HBF roadmap timing into 2026–2027. Risk disclosures emphasized potential margin sensitivity to NAND pricing normalization, execution/concentration risks related to long-term NBMs, JV reliance with Kioxia, and node-ramp/qualification timing risks. On June 8, 2026, BofA lifted its SNDK price target to $2,100 citing tight NAND supply, AI demand, and the NBM constructs.

Enterprise flash pivot and early cloud integrations

2014-06-16 → 2016-01-01
SanDisk moved to strengthen its enterprise flash position with the all-cash acquisition of Fusion-io in mid-2014, a $1.1 billion deal intended to deepen participation in hyperscale and data center storage workloads. Contemporary coverage highlighted Fusion-io’s exposure to large-scale social and consumer internet customers. In early 2016, SanDisk’s Memory Zone app documentation referenced integrations with popular consumer cloud storage services (e.g., Google Drive and OneDrive), signaling ongoing product linkages into broader cloud ecosystems at the application layer. These moves predate the current wave of AI infrastructure spend but form historical context for SanDisk’s data center and cloud-adjacent positioning.
